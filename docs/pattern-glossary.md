# Pattern Glossary — Ngā Tauira Toi

Documented pattern names, meanings, and contexts, from citable public sources (see `sources.md`). Meanings vary by iwi and context; where sources differ, both readings are kept. This glossary is knowledge, **not** a licence to use any pattern — see `TIKANGA.md` §3.

## Kōwhaiwhai — painted scroll patterns

Painted rafter (heke) and ridgepole patterns of the wharenui; built on koru/pītau and kape elements using reflection, rotation, and translational symmetry.

| Pattern | Literal | Documented associations |
|---|---|---|
| **Koru / pītau** | unfurling fern frond | Birth, growth, new life, the cycle of life; pītau is the bulbed form |
| **Kape / kape rua** | crescent(s) | Negative-space crescent; associated in some sources with the moon, or with whales |
| **Mangōpare** | hammerhead shark | Strength, courage, determination, tenacity |
| **Pātiki / pātikitiki** | flounder | Hospitality, abundance, favourable times; also a tukutuku/tāniko form |
| **Pūhoro** | — | The wake of a waka paddle; speed, swiftness, agility; ocean and rivers. Also a major moko and carving form |
| **Ngutukākā** | kākā beak | Associated with the kākā and with oratory in some traditions |
| **Kōwhai ngutukākā** | kākā-beak flower | Regional variants across rohe |
| **Rauru** | — | Spiral form associated with Ngāti Porou / East Coast kōwhaiwhai traditions |

## Tukutuku — woven lattice panels

Cross-stitched lattice panels lining wharenui walls, woven from kākaho, kiekie, pīngao, harakeke.

| Pattern | Literal | Documented associations |
|---|---|---|
| **Poutama** | stepped stairway | Whakapapa; the ascent of Tāne-o-te-wānanga to the heavens for the three baskets of knowledge; learning and aspiration |
| **Purapura whetū** | star seeds | Multitudes — stars, and the many people; also called pukanohi (herring eyes), kōwhiti |
| **Roimata toroa** | albatross tears | Misfortune, disaster, grief; vertical tear-lines |
| **Niho taniwha** | taniwha teeth | Chiefly lineage and strength; identified with Te Arawa and Waikato traditions |
| **Kaokao** | ribs/side | The braced sides and arms of warriors in haka; strength; Te Arawa and East Coast associations |
| **Pātiki** | flounder | Hospitality and abundance (as in kōwhaiwhai) |
| **Waharua kōpito** | double mouth | Points of change or convergence; meeting of people |
| **Mangōroa** | the Milky Way | The long shark of the sky in some traditions |

## Whakairo — carving surface patterns

| Pattern | Description | Documented associations |
|---|---|---|
| **Haehae** | parallel grooves | Framework of most surface decoration |
| **Pākati** | dog-tooth notches between haehae | Style marker — its shape and depth distinguish iwi styles |
| **Rauponga** | pākati rows flanked by haehae and raumoa ridges | Widespread; used in double spirals converging in S-forms |
| **Unaunahi** | fish scales | Most common in Te Tai Tokerau; called ritorito (flax shoots) in Whanganui, where it can denote priesthood (men) or noble birth (women) |
| **Taratara-ā-kae** | barbed zigzag notching | Abundance and material wealth; characteristic of pātaka (storehouses); linked to the Kae narrative |
| **Pūwerewere** | spider-web | Fine radiating pattern |
| **Rape / raperape** | buttock spirals | Used as whakapapa signifiers on carved figures |
| **Pūhoro** | split spiral | Speed and agility; shared with moko and kōwhaiwhai vocabularies |

## Tāniko — finger-woven borders

Geometric borders of kākahu (cloaks). Recurrent forms: **aramoana** (ocean path), **aronui** (pursuit of knowledge), **niho taniwha**, **pātiki**, **waharua**. Colour language traditionally black, white/natural, red-brown (tānekaha dyes), later wider palettes.

## Tā moko — for knowledge and review only

Documented public knowledge: moko encodes whakapapa, rank, and life history; **mataora** (male facial moko) and **moko kauae** (female chin moko) are the most tapu forms; body regions carry distinct design vocabularies (e.g. pūhoro across thigh/lower back). Per `TIKANGA.md` §3 this system records this knowledge to **review and educate** — it never designs moko. Design belongs to tohunga tā moko working with the wearer's whakapapa.

## Raranga — plaiting

Whāriki, kete, and their named weaves. Whakairo patterns in raranga (patterned weaving) carry names and narratives that are frequently whānau- or hapū-specific: provenance rules apply strongly here.
