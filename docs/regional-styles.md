# Regional & Iwi Design Styles — Ngā Kāinga o te Toi

Documented, citable regional distinctions. **Descriptive knowledge only** — use of an iwi-identified style requires that iwi's authority (`TIKANGA.md` §1, §3). Style boundaries are porous; carvers travelled, styles intermarried, and post-1860s wharenui mixed traditions deliberately.

## Te Tai Tokerau (Northland — incl. Ngāpuhi, Ngāti Whātua, Te Rarawa, Te Aupōuri, Ngāti Kahu)

- **Serpentine style**: figures with cone-like heads and long, sinuous S-shaped bodies, often tubular and sparsely surface-carved.
- **Unaunahi** (fish scales) is the most prevalent surface pattern in the north.
- Northern waka taua carving and pare traditions carry distinctive spiral treatments.
- *For Brett's rohe: this is the style family of Te Tai Tokerau projects — local hapū variation still applies marae to marae.*

## Te Arawa (Rotorua lakes)

- The dominant "classic" style in public perception, via the 19th–20th c. carving schools and Te Puia/NZMACI lineage.
- Internal distinctions: **Ngāti Whakaue** (lineage of the ancestor-carver Pūkākī), **Ngāti Tarāwhai** (wider, bolder haehae with smaller pākati; Anaha Te Rahui, Tene Waitere), **Ngāti Pikiao** (deep pākati).
- Square-shouldered figures, strong surface decoration, developed spiral work.

## Tairāwhiti / Ngāti Porou (East Coast)

- **Iwirākau** carving tradition; three-sided pākati more often than the balanced Te Arawa pākati.
- **Rauru** spiral kōwhaiwhai traditions; the great figurative kōwhaiwhai innovations of Rongopai (Tūranga).
- Whare of note: Te Hau-ki-Tūranga (the oldest extant carved meeting house), Porourangi.

## Mātaatua (Bay of Plenty — Ngāti Awa, Tūhoe, Whakatōhea)

- Surface patterns close to Te Arawa with pākati differences; strong figurative pare traditions.
- Mataatua wharenui (Whakatāne) a key reference.

## Taranaki / Te Āti Awa

- Serpentine figures with peaked foreheads read as homage to the maunga Taranaki.
- **Ritorito** (clustered unaunahi); deep modelling, intertwined limbs.
- Distinctive taurapa and pare forms.

## Whanganui

- Documented local names and readings for shared patterns (ritorito for unaunahi) and its own carving āhuatanga documented by the Whanganui Regional Museum.

## Waikato / Tainui

- Niho taniwha strongly identified with Tainui identity (also Te Arawa); Kīngitanga-era painted and carved innovation, e.g. at Tūrangawaewae.

## Te Waipounamu (South Island — Ngāi Tahu)

- Older, sparser carving traditions; strong rock-art (tuhituhi neherā) heritage in limestone shelters; distinctive weaving traditions (pīngao use).
- Contemporary Ngāi Tahu design identity actively curated by the iwi — engage Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu channels.

## Working rule for agents

Attribute style observations to the **rohe level documented in sources**; never invent hapū-level attributions. When a project is for a specific marae, hapū, or iwi, the correct authority is that community, and the significance layer must flag engagement as a required step.
